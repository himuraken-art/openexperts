The VMware Exit Playbook for Business Owners

A step-by-step guide to evaluating your options, planning your migration, and making the business case — written for decision-makers, not engineers.

If you're reading this, you're probably staring at a VMware renewal quote that looks nothing like last year's. You're not imagining it. Since Broadcom acquired VMware in late 2023, licensing costs have increased dramatically — 2x to 10x in many cases. The free ESXi tier that thousands of small businesses relied on is gone. Perpetual licenses have been eliminated in favor of subscriptions. And the bundling changes mean you're often forced to pay for features you don't use.

This playbook is designed to help you think through the decision clearly, plan a migration if that's the right move, and avoid the common mistakes that turn a straightforward transition into a costly mess. Everything here comes from real-world experience helping businesses navigate this exact situation.

For a deeper technical comparison of VMware alternatives, see our blog post on VMware alternatives for small business.

Section 1: Understanding Your Current Position

Before you can make a good decision about where to go, you need a clear picture of where you are. Work through these questions — the answers will drive everything that follows.

What VMware products are you running?

  • ESXi (free or licensed?) — the hypervisor itself
  • vSphere — the management layer
  • vCenter — centralized management for multiple hosts
  • vSAN — software-defined storage
  • NSX — network virtualization
  • Other VMware products (SRM, Horizon, etc.)

If you're running just ESXi on one or two hosts with no vCenter, your migration is straightforward. If you're running a full vSphere cluster with vSAN and NSX, it's more complex — but still very doable.

What's your current inventory?

  • How many physical hosts (servers)?
  • How many virtual machines (VMs)?
  • Total storage in use?
  • What operating systems are your VMs running? (Windows Server, Linux, etc.)
  • Which VMs are critical (can't be down for more than an hour)?
  • Which VMs could tolerate a few hours of downtime during migration?

What are you spending?

  • Current annual licensing cost (VMware licenses + support)
  • Your renewal quote from Broadcom/VMware
  • Any third-party tools that are VMware-specific (backup software, monitoring, etc.)

When is your renewal?

This is your deadline. If renewal is 6+ months out, you have time to do this right. If it's 2-3 months, you need to move faster but can still make it. If it's next month, you may need to negotiate a short-term renewal while you plan your exit.

What features are you actually using?

Many businesses pay for vSphere Enterprise Plus but only use features available in Standard. Some pay for vSAN but have traditional storage that works fine. Be honest about what you actually use versus what you're licensed for. This matters because the alternative platforms may not replicate every VMware feature — but if you're not using those features anyway, it doesn't matter.

Section 2: Your Options (Honest Assessment)

There are several viable alternatives to VMware. Here's what you need to know about each one — including when they're the right fit and when they're not.

Proxmox VE is our recommendation for most small and mid-size businesses. It's a mature, full-featured virtualization platform based on Debian Linux. You get clustering, live migration, built-in backup, ZFS storage, software-defined networking, and a web-based management console — all for free. Optional paid support subscriptions start at around $100/year per CPU socket if you want enterprise support and access to the tested update repository. We've written extensively about Proxmox on our Proxmox migration page.

XCP-ng is an open-source fork of Citrix XenServer. It's solid, stable, and has a good management tool (Xen Orchestra). The community is smaller than Proxmox's, which means fewer online resources when you're troubleshooting. It's a good option if your team has XenServer experience or if you prefer the Xen hypervisor architecture.

Hyper-V is still available as a role in Windows Server, but Microsoft has been quietly deprioritizing it in favor of Azure. The free Hyper-V Server product was discontinued. You still need Windows Server licenses, so the cost savings over VMware are real but not as dramatic as moving to a free platform. If you're already heavily invested in Microsoft (System Center, Azure AD, etc.), Hyper-V can be a natural fit.

Nutanix AHV is an enterprise-grade hypervisor that comes with Nutanix's hyper-converged infrastructure platform. It's powerful, but the hardware requirements and overall cost put it firmly in enterprise territory. If you're a 500+ person company with dedicated IT staff and budget to match, it's worth evaluating. For most small businesses, it's overkill.

Staying on VMware is sometimes the right call. If you're locked into a long-term agreement with favorable pricing, if you have specific VMware-only integrations that would be costly to replace, or if your team's VMware expertise is deep and retraining would be significant — staying may make sense. But go in with your eyes open: Broadcom has shown no signs of reversing course on pricing, and the trend is toward more bundling, higher costs, and less flexibility.

Section 3: Building Your Business Case

If you've decided an alternative looks promising, the next step is building a business case that justifies the migration. Here's a simple framework.

Current VMware Costs (Annual)

  • VMware licensing and support: $________
  • VMware-specific third-party tools (backup, monitoring): $________
  • Staff time managing VMware-specific issues: $________
  • Total current annual cost: $________

Migration Costs (One-Time)

  • Hardware changes (if any — often none): $________
  • Migration labor (internal staff time or external consultant): $________
  • Training for IT staff on new platform: $________
  • Optional support subscription for new platform: $________
  • Potential downtime cost during migration: $________
  • Total one-time migration cost: $________

New Platform Costs (Annual)

  • Platform licensing (Proxmox/XCP-ng: $0, Hyper-V: Windows Server license): $________
  • Optional support subscription: $________
  • Replacement tools for any VMware-specific software: $________
  • Total new annual cost: $________

Breakeven Analysis

Annual savings = Current annual cost minus New annual cost. Breakeven = One-time migration cost divided by Annual savings. For most small businesses migrating from VMware to Proxmox, the breakeven point is 3-6 months. After that, the savings are pure upside — year after year.

Beyond the raw numbers, factor in the intangible benefits: elimination of vendor lock-in, predictable costs (no more renewal surprises), and the ability to allocate budget to things that actually grow your business instead of feeding a licensing machine.

Section 4: Migration Planning Framework

A VMware migration isn't something you rush. Here's a phased approach that we've refined over many migrations. Adjust the timelines based on your environment size and complexity.

Phase 1: Assessment (2-4 weeks)

  • Complete inventory of all hosts, VMs, storage, and networking
  • Map dependencies between VMs (which VMs talk to which, what breaks if something moves)
  • Identify quick wins: standalone VMs with no complex dependencies that can migrate first
  • Identify complex migrations: VMs with specific hardware requirements, clustered applications, or VMware-specific tools
  • Document current backup and disaster recovery procedures
  • Evaluate target platform and confirm it meets all requirements

Phase 2: Proof of Concept (2-4 weeks)

  • Set up the target platform in a test environment (can be on existing hardware or a single test server)
  • Migrate 2-3 non-critical VMs to the new platform
  • Validate all functionality: networking, storage performance, backup, monitoring
  • Let your IT team get hands-on experience with the new management tools
  • Document any issues and solutions
  • Decide: go or no-go for pilot phase

Phase 3: Pilot (2-4 weeks)

  • Migrate a batch of production VMs (start with the quick wins from Phase 1)
  • Run in parallel — keep the VMware originals available as fallback
  • Validate performance under real production workloads
  • Test backup and recovery procedures on the new platform
  • Get feedback from users and IT staff
  • Refine the migration process based on lessons learned

Phase 4: Production Migration (4-8 weeks)

  • Systematic migration of remaining VMs, working through your priority list
  • Schedule maintenance windows for critical VMs that need brief downtime
  • Test every VM after migration before marking it complete
  • Update DNS, firewall rules, monitoring, and backup jobs as VMs move
  • Keep VMware environment running as fallback throughout this phase
  • Document everything as you go

Phase 5: Decommission (2 weeks)

  • Confirm all VMs are running successfully on the new platform
  • Verify all backups are working correctly
  • Run the old VMware environment in standby for a final validation period
  • Shut down and decommission VMware infrastructure
  • Cancel VMware licenses (time this with your renewal date)
  • Complete final documentation

Total timeline: 12-22 weeks. That's 3-5 months for a complete migration. For smaller environments (1-2 hosts, under 20 VMs), it can be done in 6-8 weeks. The key is not rushing. Every shortcut you take during migration is a problem you'll deal with later.

Section 5: Risk Mitigation

Migration risk is real, but it's manageable. The businesses that get into trouble are the ones that skip steps to save time. Here are the non-negotiable rules:

Always run parallel. Never decommission the VMware environment until the new platform has been running in production for at least 2 weeks with no issues. Keep the old VMs available as fallback. Yes, this means running both environments simultaneously for a while. That's the cost of safety, and it's worth it.

Full backups before every step. Before you migrate any VM, verify you have a complete, tested backup. Not just a backup job that says "successful" — a backup you've actually restored and verified. If a migration goes wrong, your backup is your safety net. Don't proceed without it.

Keep VMware running during migration. This seems obvious, but some businesses try to save money by letting their VMware licenses lapse before the migration is complete. Don't do this. If something goes wrong mid-migration, you need to be able to fall back to the known-good VMware environment. Budget for the overlap period.

Have a rollback plan for every VM. Before migrating each VM, document exactly how to roll it back to VMware if needed. This usually means keeping the VMware copy intact until the migration is validated. If you're converting VM formats, keep the original VMDK files until you're confident.

Test, test, test before going live. After migrating a VM, don't just check that it boots. Test the actual application running on it. Can users connect? Does the database respond? Are the integrations working? Are network connections to other VMs and services functioning? A VM that boots but doesn't work correctly is worse than one that clearly failed — because you might not notice the problem until it matters.

Section 6: Communicating with Stakeholders

A successful migration isn't just a technical project — it's a change management exercise. Here's how to communicate it effectively.

For leadership / the board / the owner:

Focus on the business case. Use language like this:

  • "Our VMware licensing costs have increased [X]% due to Broadcom's acquisition. Our current renewal is $[amount], up from $[previous amount]."
  • "We've evaluated alternatives and identified [platform] as a viable replacement that will reduce our annual virtualization costs from $[current] to $[new]."
  • "The migration will take approximately [X] weeks and cost approximately $[amount] in labor and training. We'll break even within [X] months."
  • "Risk is managed through a phased approach with parallel operation and full rollback capability at every stage."
  • "This also eliminates our dependency on a single vendor that has demonstrated it will raise prices unpredictably."

For staff:

Most staff won't notice any difference. The virtualization platform runs underneath the servers and applications they use. Your communication should be brief and reassuring:

  • "We're upgrading our server infrastructure over the next [X] weeks."
  • "You may experience brief maintenance windows for [specific systems] during [specific dates]. We'll notify you in advance."
  • "Day-to-day, nothing changes for you. Same applications, same login, same everything."
  • "If you notice anything unusual after a maintenance window, please contact [IT contact] immediately."

The key message for everyone: this is a planned, controlled change driven by cost optimization, not a crisis. You're moving proactively, not reactively.

Section 7: Choosing a Migration Partner

Unless you have deep in-house expertise with both VMware and your target platform, working with an experienced migration partner will save you time, money, and stress. Here's what to look for.

What matters:

  • Experience with both platforms. They need to know VMware well enough to understand what you're migrating from, and the target platform well enough to set it up correctly. Ask for specific examples of similar migrations.
  • Small business experience. Enterprise migration consultants may be technically excellent but unfamiliar with the constraints and priorities of small business. You need someone who understands that budget matters and downtime isn't an option.
  • A clear, documented process. They should be able to walk you through their migration methodology step by step. If they can't explain the process clearly, they haven't done enough of these.
  • References you can actually call. Not case studies on a website — real businesses you can talk to about their experience.

Questions to ask:

  • How many VMware-to-[target platform] migrations have you completed?
  • What's the largest environment you've migrated? The smallest?
  • What's your rollback process if something goes wrong?
  • How do you handle the parallel operation period?
  • What's included in your quote, and what costs extra?
  • Will you provide documentation and training so our team can manage it going forward?

Red flags:

  • They push a specific platform without understanding your environment first
  • They can't provide references from completed migrations
  • They minimize the complexity or promise unrealistic timelines
  • They don't mention parallel operation, rollback plans, or testing
  • They want to lock you into ongoing management instead of training your team

The best migration partner is one who wants to do the project, train your team, and hand you the keys. You shouldn't need them for ongoing management unless you want that — and you definitely shouldn't be locked into it.
