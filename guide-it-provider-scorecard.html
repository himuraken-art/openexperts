IT Provider Scorecard: Rate Your Provider

10 categories. 100 points. An honest picture of whether your IT provider is doing their job — built from 30+ years of seeing what good and bad IT actually looks like.

How to Use This Scorecard

This scorecard is designed to give you an objective look at your IT provider's performance. It's not a gotcha tool or a setup for a sales pitch. It's a straightforward evaluation based on what we've learned matters most over three decades of working with small businesses.

For each of the 10 categories below, answer the questions honestly. Give yourself the points listed for each "yes" answer. Skip the questions that don't apply to your situation. At the end, add up your total and check the scoring guide for an honest interpretation.

A few ground rules:

  • "I don't know" counts as "no." If you don't know whether your provider is doing something, they're either not doing it or not communicating about it — and both are problems.
  • Be honest. The only person this scorecard helps is you. Generous scoring defeats the purpose.
  • This isn't about perfection. No provider scores 100. The goal is to identify strengths and gaps so you can have a productive conversation — either with your current provider or a new one.
Category 1 — Up to 10 Points

Responsiveness

When something breaks, how fast does your IT provider respond? Not "how fast do they promise to respond" in a contract — how fast do they actually pick up the phone, acknowledge the issue, and start working on it?

Question Points Your Score
Do they respond to critical issues within 30 minutes or less?3
Do they respond to routine requests within 4 hours or less?3
Do they provide after-hours or emergency support?2
Do they have a clear escalation process for unresolved issues?2

Responsiveness is the most visible measure of IT quality. If your provider takes hours to acknowledge a critical problem, everything else on this scorecard matters less. When your systems are down, your business is losing money, and response time is the only thing that determines how much.

Category 2 — Up to 10 Points

Documentation

Good IT providers document everything about your environment. Great IT providers make sure you have access to that documentation. If your provider got hit by a bus tomorrow, could someone else step in and understand your systems?

Question Points Your Score
Do they maintain up-to-date documentation of your network, servers, and systems?3
Do YOU have access to that documentation (not just them)?3
Do they provide network diagrams showing how your systems connect?2
Do they document changes they make to your environment?2

Documentation is one of the most overlooked aspects of IT service. Many providers keep everything in their heads or in their own systems — which means you're dependent on them whether their service is good or not. That's not a partnership. That's a trap. Your documentation, passwords, and system information belong to you.

Category 3 — Up to 10 Points

Security Practices

Security isn't optional. It's a fundamental part of IT management. If your provider has never had a serious conversation with you about cybersecurity, that should concern you.

Question Points Your Score
Do they manage and monitor your firewall?3
Have they implemented or enforced multi-factor authentication (MFA) on your accounts?3
Do they apply security patches and updates regularly (monthly at minimum)?2
Have they conducted a security review or assessment in the past 12 months?2

Small businesses are the number one target for cyberattacks because attackers know small businesses often have weak security. A good IT provider makes security part of the baseline service, not an expensive add-on. If security is an "extra" they charge for on top of your managed services, ask why protecting your business isn't part of managing it.

Category 4 — Up to 10 Points

Backup & Recovery

Backups are the last line of defense. If everything else fails — ransomware, hardware failure, human error — your backups are what keep your business alive. But backups that aren't tested are backups you can't trust.

Question Points Your Score
Are your critical systems backed up at least daily?3
Do they test backup restores regularly (and can they show you the results)?3
Do you have offsite or cloud copies of your backups (separate from your main location)?2
Are your Microsoft 365 or Google Workspace emails backed up separately (not just relying on Microsoft/Google)?2

We've walked into businesses that were told they had backups — only to find the backup job had been failing for months with no one monitoring it. Tested, verified, monitored backups are different from backups that are "set up." Ask your provider when the last successful restore test was. If they can't give you a date, you have a problem.

Category 5 — Up to 10 Points

Communication

Your IT provider should communicate with you like a business partner, not a mystery. You should understand what they're doing, why they're doing it, and what it means for your business.

Question Points Your Score
Do they explain technical issues in plain English you can actually understand?3
Do they have regular check-ins or business reviews with you (at least quarterly)?3
Do they explain what they're going to do before they do it (rather than making changes without telling you)?2
Do they proactively notify you about issues, upcoming changes, or things that need attention?2

IT providers who hide behind jargon are either trying to sound important or trying to keep you from understanding what you're paying for. Neither is acceptable. You deserve to understand your own technology in terms that make sense to you. If they can't explain it simply, they may not understand it well enough themselves.

Category 6 — Up to 10 Points

Cost Transparency

You should know exactly what you're paying for. Surprise invoices, vague line items, and unpredictable bills are signs that something is off — either in the provider's billing practices or in how the engagement was set up.

Question Points Your Score
Are invoices itemized and easy to understand?3
Is your monthly bill predictable (no surprise charges)?3
Do they clearly communicate what costs extra before doing the work?2
Do they help you plan and budget for upcoming technology needs?2

Unpredictable IT costs make it impossible to budget properly. A good provider gives you a clear monthly cost for standard services and communicates any additional charges before the work happens — not after. If you dread opening your IT invoice because you never know what it's going to be, that's a billing relationship problem, not just a cost problem.

Category 7 — Up to 10 Points

Proactive vs. Reactive

The most expensive way to run IT is to wait for things to break and then pay someone to fix them. A good IT provider prevents problems. A mediocre one just responds to them.

Question Points Your Score
Do they actively monitor your systems for issues (not just wait for you to report them)?3
Do they perform preventive maintenance (updates, disk cleanup, hardware checks) on a regular schedule?3
Do they track hardware age and recommend replacements before things fail?2
Have they ever presented you with a technology roadmap or plan for the next 1-2 years?2

Here's a simple test: do you mostly hear from your IT provider when something is broken, or do they reach out with recommendations, updates, and plans? If the only time they contact you is when there's a problem (or an invoice), they're reactive. Proactive IT costs less in the long run because you're paying for prevention instead of emergencies.

Category 8 — Up to 10 Points

Vendor Independence

This category is about whether you could leave your IT provider tomorrow if you needed to. Not whether you want to — whether you could. A good provider makes it easy to leave because they're confident you won't want to.

Question Points Your Score
Do you have all admin passwords for your own systems, accounts, and services?3
Do you have access to complete, current documentation of your environment?3
Could you switch to a different IT provider within 30 days without losing data or access?2
Are you free from long-term contracts with heavy cancellation penalties?2

If the idea of switching providers feels terrifying — not because change is hard, but because you don't have your own passwords, documentation, or access — that's a serious problem. Whether it was intentional or just sloppy, the result is the same: you're locked in. Your systems, your data, your passwords, and your documentation belong to you. Period.

Category 9 — Up to 10 Points

Strategic Guidance

A good IT provider doesn't just keep the lights on. They help you make smart technology decisions that support your business goals. They think ahead so you don't get blindsided by outdated systems or missed opportunities.

Question Points Your Score
Do they help you plan technology purchases and upgrades (not just sell you things)?3
Do they help you create or maintain a technology budget?3
Do they proactively recommend solutions to business problems (not just technology problems)?2
Do they understand your industry and the specific challenges your business faces?2

There's a big difference between an IT provider who keeps your computers running and one who helps your business run better. Strategic guidance means they understand where your business is going and help technology get you there. If your provider has never asked about your business plans, growth goals, or biggest operational challenges, they're a technician, not a partner.

Category 10 — Up to 10 Points

Overall Relationship

Sometimes the most important measure is the simplest one. After all the checkboxes and technical questions, how do you actually feel about your IT provider?

Question Points Your Score
Do you trust your IT provider to act in your best interest?3
Would you recommend them to another business owner?3
Do they understand your business — not just your technology?2
Do you feel like a valued client, regardless of your company's size?2

Trust is the foundation of every good IT relationship. If you don't trust your provider — if you wonder whether they're recommending things because you need them or because they want to sell them — that erodes everything. And if you wouldn't recommend them to a friend, ask yourself why you're still using them.

Your Results

Scoring Guide

Add up your points from all 10 categories. Your total is out of 100. Here's what it means.

80 – 100 Points: Great Provider

You've got a good one. Your IT provider is responsive, transparent, proactive, and treats your business like a real partner. That doesn't mean they're perfect — nobody is — but the foundation is solid. Look at any categories where you lost points and have a conversation about them. A great provider will appreciate the feedback and work to close the gaps. Don't take this relationship for granted — good IT providers are harder to find than most people realize.

60 – 79 Points: Room for Improvement

Your provider is doing some things well, but there are meaningful gaps. This is the range where a direct conversation can make a real difference. Share the specific areas where you scored low and ask how they plan to address them. Some gaps might be easy fixes — maybe they have documentation but haven't shared it with you, or they do backup testing but haven't been reporting the results. Give them a chance to improve. If they're receptive and make changes, great. If they get defensive or nothing changes in 90 days, it's time to start looking.

40 – 59 Points: Serious Gaps

This score means your IT provider is underperforming in fundamental areas. You're likely not getting the security, communication, or proactive management your business needs. At this level, the risk isn't just inconvenience — it's real business exposure. An unmonitored backup, an unmanaged firewall, or a provider you can't reach in an emergency can turn a routine problem into a catastrophe. Have an honest conversation with your provider, but also start evaluating alternatives. You deserve better, and better is available.

Below 40 Points: Time for a Change

There's no gentle way to put this: if your IT provider scores below 40, they're not managing your IT in any meaningful way. You're likely paying for a reactive break-fix service dressed up as managed IT. Your systems are probably not properly secured, your backups may not be reliable, and you likely couldn't switch providers without significant disruption — which may be why you haven't. But staying with a provider who isn't doing the job is more expensive than switching. The risk you're carrying right now — in security, in data loss, in downtime — is real and compounding. It's time to make a change.
