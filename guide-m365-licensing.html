Microsoft 365 Licensing Cheat Sheet

A plain-English guide to picking the right M365 plans for your small business — and stop paying for features nobody uses.

Microsoft 365 licensing is one of those things that should be simple but isn't. Microsoft offers dozens of plans with overlapping names, constantly shifting features, and pricing that requires a spreadsheet to decode. If you've ever stared at a Microsoft licensing page and felt your eyes glaze over, you're not alone.

Here's the thing: most small businesses are overpaying. Not by a little — by 20-30%. They put everyone on the same plan, never revisit the decision, and don't realize there's a smarter way. This guide walks you through exactly what each plan includes, who needs what, and how to save real money without cutting corners on security.

The Plans That Matter for Small Business

Microsoft offers a confusing array of plans, but for small businesses (under 300 users), there are really only three that matter. Everything else is either enterprise-focused or too niche to worry about.

Microsoft 365 Business Basic — $6/user/month

This is your entry-level plan. You get web and mobile versions of Office apps (Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Outlook), but not the desktop applications. You also get Exchange Online email, 1TB of OneDrive storage, Teams, and SharePoint. That's it — and for many users, that's enough.

Who needs Basic? People who primarily use email and Teams on their phone or in a web browser. Think warehouse workers, field technicians, retail staff, part-time employees, or anyone who doesn't spend their day creating Word documents and Excel spreadsheets on a computer. If they just need to check email, join Teams calls, and access shared files, Basic does the job.

Microsoft 365 Business Standard — $12.50/user/month

Standard includes everything in Basic, plus the full desktop versions of Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Outlook, and the rest of the Office suite. You can install them on up to 5 PCs or Macs, 5 tablets, and 5 phones per user. You also get some additional tools like Bookings, Forms, and the ability to host webinars.

Who needs Standard? Anyone who does real work in Office applications on a computer. If they're creating documents, building spreadsheets, making presentations, or managing email heavily in the desktop Outlook app, they need Standard. This is where most of your office-based staff should land.

Microsoft 365 Business Premium — $22/user/month

Premium includes everything in Standard, plus a serious set of security and device management tools: Intune (mobile device management), Defender for Business (endpoint security), Azure AD Premium P1 (conditional access policies), Information Protection, and Data Loss Prevention. This is where the security features live.

Who needs Premium? People who handle sensitive data — financial records, customer personal information, healthcare data, legal documents. Also executives, because they're high-value targets for phishing and social engineering. And anyone who accesses company data from personal devices, because Intune lets you manage and protect company data on those devices without controlling the whole phone.

Common Licensing Mistakes

After auditing Microsoft 365 licensing for hundreds of small businesses, the same mistakes come up over and over. Here are the ones that cost the most money:

Putting everyone on Premium when only a few people need it. This is the most expensive mistake. If you have 30 employees and put everyone on Premium at $22/user/month, you're spending $7,920/year. If only 8 of those people actually need the security features (executives, finance, HR), and the rest can use Standard or Basic, you could be spending $4,800-5,400 instead. That's $2,500-3,000/year in savings — every year.

Keeping everyone on Standard when some people only need email. If you have warehouse workers, drivers, retail staff, or part-timers who just check email and join the occasional Teams call, they don't need desktop Office apps. Moving them from Standard to Basic saves $6.50/user/month. For 10 such users, that's $780/year.

Not realizing Basic doesn't include desktop Office apps. This one burns people the other direction. They see the low price, put everyone on Basic, and then get calls from staff who can't open Word documents properly or whose Excel files don't render right in the browser. The web apps are good, but they're not the full desktop apps. If someone needs to do real work in Office, they need Standard or Premium.

Ignoring Premium for people who handle sensitive data. Saving a few dollars per user by skipping Premium for your bookkeeper, your HR manager, or your CEO is a false economy. Those security features — conditional access, Defender for Business, Intune — are specifically designed to protect the people and data most at risk. A single phishing incident targeting your CFO will cost more than a decade of Premium licensing.

Paying for E3 or E5 enterprise plans when Business plans work fine. Some IT providers default to Enterprise plans because that's what they know. Enterprise E3 starts at $36/user/month. Enterprise E5 is $57/user/month. Unless you have over 300 users or need very specific enterprise features (like eDiscovery Premium, advanced compliance, or Microsoft Defender for Office 365 Plan 2), the Business plans do everything you need at a fraction of the cost.

The Mix-and-Match Strategy

Here's what most IT providers won't tell you: you don't have to put everyone on the same plan. Microsoft lets you assign different licenses to different users. And that's where the real savings are.

Think of it this way:

  • Executives, finance, and HR: Premium ($22/user/month) — they handle sensitive data, they're high-value phishing targets, and the security features are worth every penny.
  • General office staff: Standard ($12.50/user/month) — they need desktop Office apps to do their jobs, but they don't need the advanced security layer.
  • Warehouse, field, and frontline workers: Basic ($6/user/month) — they need email and Teams on their phone. That's it. Don't pay for desktop apps they'll never install.

Example: A 25-person company

Let's say you have 5 executives/finance staff, 12 general office workers, and 8 warehouse/field workers.

Everyone on Standard (the typical setup): 25 x $12.50 = $312.50/month = $3,750/year

Mix-and-match:

  • 5 Premium: 5 x $22 = $110/month
  • 12 Standard: 12 x $12.50 = $150/month
  • 8 Basic: 8 x $6 = $48/month
  • Total: $308/month = $3,696/year

In this case the monthly savings are modest — about $55/year. But consider: you've also upgraded your executives to Premium with proper security features, something the all-Standard approach didn't include. If you were on all-Premium before ($6,600/year), the savings are $2,904/year with the same security coverage where it matters.

The real win isn't just cost savings. It's getting the right level of protection for the right people at the right price. That's what optimization actually looks like.

What About Exchange Online Plans?

Sometimes you don't need the full Microsoft 365 suite. You just need email. That's where Exchange Online standalone plans come in.

Exchange Online Plan 1 — $4/user/month

You get a 50GB mailbox, web-based Outlook, and basic email protection. No Office apps, no Teams, no OneDrive, no SharePoint. Just email. This makes sense for users who only need a professional email address and don't need any of the collaboration tools.

Exchange Online Plan 2 — $8/user/month

Same as Plan 1 but with a 100GB mailbox, unlimited archiving, and Data Loss Prevention. The extra mailbox space matters for people who keep years of email. The archiving is useful for compliance-heavy industries.

Microsoft 365 F1/F3 (Frontline Worker plans)

Starting at $2.25/user/month, these are designed for shift workers, retail employees, and other frontline staff. Limited functionality, but enough for basic communication. Worth looking at if you have a large number of workers who just need the basics.

The standalone Exchange plans are niche. For most small businesses, Business Basic at $6/user gives you email plus Teams, OneDrive, and SharePoint — which is only $2 more than Exchange Online Plan 1. That extra $2 is usually worth it for the collaboration tools. But if you truly just need email and nothing else, the standalone option exists.

Add-Ons Worth Considering

Microsoft loves add-ons. Most of them aren't worth the money for small businesses. Here's the honest breakdown:

Microsoft 365 Backup — Worth it. This is a relatively new product that provides proper backup of your Exchange, OneDrive, and SharePoint data. Here's something most people don't realize: Microsoft does not back up your data for you. If someone deletes files, if ransomware encrypts your OneDrive, or if a departing employee wipes their mailbox, Microsoft's recycle bin has limited retention. A real backup solution is non-negotiable. Whether you use Microsoft's own product or a third-party like Veeam or Datto, you need this.

Teams Phone / Phone System — Situational. If you want to replace your traditional phone system with Teams-based calling, Microsoft offers this as an add-on. It works well and eliminates the need for a separate phone system. But it requires planning — you need calling plans or direct routing, and the per-user costs add up. Worth exploring when your current phone contract is up for renewal.

Visio and Project — Probably don't need them. These are legacy Microsoft products that most small businesses don't need. Visio (for diagrams and flowcharts) has free alternatives like draw.io. Project (for project management) has been largely replaced by Planner (included in M365) or tools like Asana, Monday, or Trello. Don't pay for these unless someone specifically asks for them and can explain why.

Power BI — The free version is usually enough. Power BI Pro is $10/user/month, but Power BI Desktop is free and handles most small business reporting needs. Only consider Pro if you need to share interactive dashboards across your organization. For most small businesses, exporting reports from your line-of-business applications and using Excel is perfectly fine.

Annual vs. Monthly Billing

Microsoft gives you two billing options, and the difference matters more than you might think.

Annual commitment saves roughly 20% compared to monthly billing. But you're locked in for 12 months. If you need to reduce licenses mid-year, you're still paying for them. If you need to add licenses, you can — but the new ones are prorated to your annual renewal date.

Monthly billing is flexible. Add or remove licenses any time. But you're paying a premium for that flexibility — about 20% more per user per month.

The smart strategy: Use annual commitments for your stable headcount — the employees who are going to be here all year. Use monthly billing for contractors, seasonal workers, or positions with high turnover. This gives you the cost savings where they matter and the flexibility where you need it.

One more thing: if you're buying through a Microsoft partner or reseller, ask about their cancellation and modification policies. Some partners are more flexible than others on mid-term changes. And some offer better pricing than buying direct from Microsoft.
