Email A2 — The Real Cost of Getting It Wrong

Subject: A $40,000 lesson about small business security
Preview text: This happened to a 12-person company. It started with one email.
Hey, I want to tell you about something that happened to a business I worked with. I'm keeping the details vague to protect their privacy, but the story is real. They were a 12-person company. Professional services. Good people who were focused on running their business, not thinking about cybersecurity every day. Totally understandable. One morning, an employee got an email that looked like it came from their bank. It wasn't a terrible fake, but it wasn't perfect either. They clicked the link. Entered their credentials. Within 48 hours, ransomware had encrypted their file server, their accounting system, and three years of client records. The ransom demand was $25,000. But that wasn't the real cost. Here's what gets me: the attack would have been stopped by two things they didn't have. Multi-factor authentication on their email, and a basic employee training program. That's it. Two things that cost almost nothing. I'm not telling you this to scare you. I'm telling you because this stuff is preventable. Email A3 — 3 Things Most Small Businesses Get Wrong

Send: Day 7
From: Ken @ openexperts.tech
Subject: 3 security mistakes I see in almost every small business
Preview text: Number 2 is the one that surprises people the most.
Hey, After 30+ years of working with small businesses, I've noticed the same security gaps come up again and again. Here are the three biggest ones: 1. THINKING MICROSOFT 365 BACKS UP YOUR DATA It doesn't. Not in the way you're probably thinking. Microsoft's retention policies are designed for their convenience, not yours. If an employee deletes files, if ransomware encrypts your SharePoint, or if an account gets compromised — you could be out of luck. I wrote about this in more detail here: https://openexperts.tech/blog-microsoft-365-backup.html 2. NO MULTI-FACTOR AUTHENTICATION ON BUSINESS EMAIL This is the single easiest win in cybersecurity, and a shocking number of businesses still haven't done it. Your email is the skeleton key to everything — password resets, banking, client communication. If someone gets into your email with a stolen password, MFA is the thing that stops them. If you haven't turned this on yet, do it this week. Seriously. It takes about 15 minutes per user and it's the best 15 minutes you'll ever spend on security. 3. NO PLAN FOR WHAT HAPPENS WHEN SOMETHING GOES WRONG Most small businesses don't have an incident response plan. They don't know who to call, what to disconnect, or how to communicate with clients if something happens. So when it does happen, panic takes over and the damage gets worse. You don't need a 50-page document. You need a one-page plan that answers: Who do we call? What do we shut down first? How do we tell our clients? That's it. I covered a lot of this in the cybersecurity guide you downloaded, but if you want to go deeper on any of these, just hit reply. I'm a real person and I actually read these. Ken openexperts.tech

Email B2 — The Fear of Switching

Send: Day 3
From: Ken @ openexperts.tech
Subject: The #1 thing we hear from businesses switching IT providers
Preview text: It's not about the technology. It's about the fear.
Hey, You know what we hear more than anything from businesses that are thinking about switching IT providers? "What if the transition breaks everything?" I get it. You've seen the horror stories. You've maybe lived one. The idea of somebody new coming in and touching your systems — your email, your servers, your data — when you're already frustrated with your current situation? That's a lot of trust to extend. So let me be straight with you: switching IT providers is almost never as painful as people think it's going to be. Here's why. A good provider will do a full documentation and discovery phase before they change a single thing. They'll map your network, inventory your systems, review your accounts, and build a transition plan. The actual switchover is usually the boring part because all the real work happened before it. The businesses that have the worst transitions are the ones where the new provider rushes in and starts making changes on day one. That's a red flag, not a sign of enthusiasm. If you're currently in that "we know we need to switch but we're afraid to pull the trigger" zone, here's my advice: start the conversation. Talk to a couple of providers. Ask them to walk you through exactly how they handle transitions. You'll know fast who takes it seriously and who's just telling you what you want to hear. And if your current provider is holding your documentation or credentials hostage — that's a sign you're dealing with the wrong people, and it's also more common than you'd think. The guide I sent you covers how to handle that. Ken openexperts.tech

Email B3 — What Your IT Provider Should Be Telling You

Send: Day 7
From: Ken @ openexperts.tech
Subject: What your IT provider should be telling you (but probably isn't)
Preview text: Three gaps most providers won't bring up — and why that matters.
Hey, There are a few things a good IT provider should proactively tell you about. In my experience, most don't — either because they don't know, or because fixing the problem would cut into their margins. Here are three big ones: YOUR MICROSOFT 365 DATA ISN'T BACKED UP I know I keep beating this drum, but it's that important. Microsoft's own service agreement says they recommend third-party backup. Most IT providers either don't mention this or charge you extra for it without explaining why it matters. If your provider hasn't brought this up, ask them about it this week. Here's more detail: https://openexperts.tech/blog-microsoft-365-backup.html YOU MIGHT BE OVERPAYING FOR SOFTWARE LICENSES A lot of small businesses are on enterprise-grade licensing plans they don't need. Microsoft 365 E5 when E3 (or even Business Premium) would do. VMware licenses that could be replaced with free alternatives like Proxmox. Your provider may not volunteer this information because they get kickbacks on licensing. We wrote about the VMware situation specifically: https://openexperts.tech/blog-vmware-alternatives.html YOU SHOULD HAVE YOUR OWN DOCUMENTATION Here's a test: if your IT provider disappeared tomorrow, would you know all your admin passwords? Would you have a network diagram? An inventory of your systems and when they were last updated? If the answer is no, that's a problem. Your documentation should belong to you, not your provider. A good provider will keep it updated and share it with you. If yours won't, that's a red flag. None of this is meant to trash your current provider. A lot of IT companies are genuinely doing their best. But these are the gaps I see most often, and they're the ones that end up costing businesses the most when something goes wrong. Ken openexperts.tech
