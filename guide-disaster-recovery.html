The Small Business Disaster Recovery Plan

A complete, fill-in-the-blank template you can finish in an afternoon. Written in plain English by someone who's helped hundreds of businesses prepare for — and recover from — the worst.

Why Every Business Needs a Disaster Recovery Plan

A disaster recovery plan isn't about paranoia. It's about running a responsible business. Servers fail. Ransomware happens. Pipes burst. Power goes out for days. These aren't hypothetical scenarios — they're things that happen to real businesses every week.

The difference between a bad week and a business-ending catastrophe usually comes down to one thing: did you have a plan, or were you making it up as you went?

Most small businesses don't have a DR plan. Not because they don't care, but because the process seems overwhelming. Consultants charge thousands of dollars. The templates you find online are written for enterprises with dedicated IT departments. And so the plan never gets written.

This template changes that. It's designed for small businesses with 5 to 100 employees. It uses plain language, not jargon. Every section has instructions and examples. You fill in the blanks with your own information, and by the end, you have a real, usable disaster recovery plan.

It won't be perfect. No plan is. But a simple plan you've actually completed is infinitely better than a perfect plan that only exists in your head.

Let's get started.

Section 2

System Inventory

You can't recover what you haven't documented. This section is a complete inventory of every system your business depends on. It sounds tedious, but it's one of the most valuable exercises you can do. Most business owners are surprised by how many systems they actually rely on once they write them all down.

For each system, document what it does, where it lives, how critical it is, how it's backed up, and how quickly you need it back if it goes down.

Section 3

Recovery Priority Matrix

When everything is down, you can't fix everything at once. This matrix tells you what to restore first, second, and third. It prevents the chaos of trying to do everything simultaneously and ensures the most important systems come back online first.

Understanding RTO and RPO (in Plain English)

Two terms you'll see in any DR conversation. Here's what they actually mean:

RTO — Recovery Time Objective: How long can this system be down before it causes serious damage to the business? If your email can be down for 2 hours but not 8, your RTO for email is 2 hours. This is your deadline for getting it back.

RPO — Recovery Point Objective: How much data can you afford to lose? If your server backs up every night at midnight and crashes at 5 PM, you lose a full day of work. If that's unacceptable, you need more frequent backups. Your RPO tells you how often to back up.

Together, RTO and RPO help you decide how much to invest in backup and recovery for each system. A system with a 1-hour RTO and a 15-minute RPO needs fast recovery and frequent backups. A system with a 48-hour RTO and a 24-hour RPO needs less investment.

Section 4

Step-by-Step Recovery Procedures

This is the core of your plan. For each type of disaster, you need a clear set of steps that anyone on your team can follow. You're not writing a technical manual — you're writing a checklist that keeps people calm and moving in the right direction during a crisis.

Below are templates for the five most common disaster scenarios. Customize each one with your specific systems, contacts, and procedures.

Scenario 1: Server Failure

A critical server stops working — hardware failure, software crash, or corruption.

  • Step 1: Confirm the server is down and identify which systems are affected. Check if the issue is the server itself or the network.
  • Step 2: Contact IT provider immediately: ___ (name) at ___ (phone number).
  • Step 3: Notify affected staff: "The [system name] is currently down. We're working on it. Use [workaround] in the meantime."
  • Step 4: IT provider assesses damage and begins recovery from most recent backup. Backup location: ___.
  • Step 5: If hardware replacement is needed, source from: ___ (vendor) or use spare: ___ (location of spare equipment).
  • Step 6: Restore data from backup. Verify data integrity before allowing users back on the system.
  • Step 7: Document what happened, what was lost (if anything), and what needs to change to prevent recurrence.

Scenario 2: Network Outage

Internet or internal network goes down. No one can access email, cloud apps, or shared files.

  • Step 1: Determine scope. Is it the internet connection, the internal network, or both? Check if the issue affects all locations or just one.
  • Step 2: Contact ISP: ___ (provider) at ___ (support number). Account number: ___.
  • Step 3: If internet is down, activate backup internet connection (if available): ___ (details).
  • Step 4: If internal network, contact IT provider: ___ (name) at ___ (phone number).
  • Step 5: Notify staff of expected downtime and provide workaround instructions (mobile hotspots, work from home, etc.).
  • Step 6: Once restored, verify all systems are functioning. Check VPN connections, VoIP phones, and cloud service access.
  • Step 7: Document the cause, duration of outage, and any prevention measures needed.

Scenario 3: Ransomware Attack

Files are encrypted, ransom demand appears, or systems are behaving suspiciously.

  • Step 1: IMMEDIATELY disconnect the affected computer(s) from the network. Unplug the ethernet cable and turn off Wi-Fi. Do NOT turn off the computer.
  • Step 2: Contact IT provider emergency line: ___ (phone number). This is a critical incident.
  • Step 3: Do NOT pay the ransom. Do NOT try to decrypt files yourself. Do NOT click on any ransom links.
  • Step 4: Notify decision maker: ___ (name) at ___ (phone). They need to authorize response actions and spending.
  • Step 5: IT provider assesses the scope of the attack — which systems are affected, whether backups are intact.
  • Step 6: If backups are clean, begin restore process according to priority matrix. If backups may be compromised, IT provider will verify before restoring.
  • Step 7: Report the incident to law enforcement: FBI's IC3 (ic3.gov) and your local police department.
  • Step 8: Contact insurance agent: ___ (name) at ___ (phone). Policy number: ___. Many policies cover cyber incidents.
  • Step 9: After recovery, conduct a thorough review. How did the attacker get in? What needs to change? Document everything.

Scenario 4: Natural Disaster / Physical Damage

Fire, flood, severe weather, or other physical damage to your office or equipment.

  • Step 1: Ensure everyone is safe. People first, always. Follow your evacuation procedures.
  • Step 2: Contact emergency services if needed (911).
  • Step 3: Once safe, contact insurance agent: ___ (name) at ___ (phone). Document all damage with photos before cleanup.
  • Step 4: Contact IT provider: ___ (name) at ___ (phone). Assess technology damage and begin recovery planning.
  • Step 5: Activate offsite backup recovery. Backup location: ___. Cloud backup provider: ___.
  • Step 6: If the office is unusable, activate remote work plan. Staff should access cloud systems from home using: ___ (instructions).
  • Step 7: Contact landlord/building management: ___ (name) at ___ (phone) for building status and timeline.
  • Step 8: Communicate with clients and vendors about the situation and expected timeline using the communication plan below.

Scenario 5: Data Corruption or Accidental Deletion

Critical files are corrupted, accidentally deleted, or a database becomes damaged.

  • Step 1: Stop using the affected system immediately. Continued use can overwrite deleted data and make recovery harder.
  • Step 2: Contact IT provider: ___ (name) at ___ (phone). Describe what was lost and when it was last known to be good.
  • Step 3: IT provider identifies the most recent clean backup. Backup location: ___.
  • Step 4: Restore the affected files or database from backup. Verify integrity before resuming use.
  • Step 5: Determine what data was created between the last backup and the incident. Notify affected staff so they can re-enter or recreate work.
  • Step 6: Review backup frequency. If the data loss gap is unacceptable, increase backup frequency for that system.
Section 5

Communication Plan

During a disaster, clear communication prevents panic, keeps people informed, and protects your reputation. The biggest mistake businesses make isn't the disaster itself — it's going silent while everyone wonders what's happening.

Your communication plan answers three questions: who tells who, what do they say, and when do they say it.

Internal Communications

Who communicates: ___ (name/role) is the primary internal communicator. If unavailable: ___ (backup name/role).

How to reach staff if email is down:

  • Group text message to: ___ (phone tree or group chat)
  • Personal cell numbers of all managers on file at: ___ (location)
  • Backup communication tool: ___ (e.g., WhatsApp group, Signal group, phone tree)

Template message — initial notification:

"We are currently experiencing a [type of incident] that is affecting [systems/services]. Our IT team is working on the issue. We expect to have more information by [time]. In the meantime, please [specific instructions — e.g., do not attempt to log in, work from home, contact clients by phone]. Updates will follow every [timeframe]."

Template message — resolution:

"The [type of incident] has been resolved. All systems are back online as of [time]. If you experience any issues, please contact [IT contact]. Thank you for your patience."

External Communications

Who communicates externally: ___ (name/role). No one else speaks to clients, vendors, or media without approval.

Template message — clients:

"We are currently experiencing a technical issue that may temporarily affect [specific service]. We are actively working to resolve it and expect normal operations to resume by [time/date]. We apologize for any inconvenience and appreciate your patience. If you need immediate assistance, please contact us at [backup phone number]."

Template message — vendors:

"We are experiencing a [brief description] and may have limited access to [email/systems] for the next [timeframe]. Please contact us by phone at [number] for urgent matters."

Communication schedule during an active incident:

  • Initial notification: Within 30 minutes of incident confirmation
  • Updates to staff: Every ___ hours (recommended: every 2 hours minimum)
  • Updates to clients (if affected): Every ___ hours
  • Resolution notification: Within 1 hour of full recovery
  • Post-incident summary: Within 48 hours

Section 7

Testing Your Plan

A disaster recovery plan that's never been tested is a disaster recovery plan that probably won't work. Testing doesn't have to be complicated or disruptive. It just needs to happen regularly.

There are three types of tests, and you should do all of them:

Tabletop exercise — Sit down with your team and walk through a scenario on paper. "The server just died. What do we do first? Who do we call? Where's the backup?" No technology involved, just people talking through the plan. This reveals gaps in communication, missing contacts, and unclear procedures. Do this quarterly.

Backup restore test — Actually recover data from your backup. Restore a file, a folder, or an entire system to a test environment. Verify the data is complete and usable. This proves your backups work. Do this monthly.

Full recovery test — Simulate a real disaster. Pretend the server is gone and actually restore it from backup to replacement hardware. Time it. Document the problems you encounter. This is the gold standard. Do this annually.

Section 8

Plan Maintenance

A disaster recovery plan is a living document. If you wrote it a year ago and haven't looked at it since, it's probably already out of date. People leave, vendors change, new systems get added, old ones get retired. Your plan needs to keep up.

Review Schedule

  • Monthly: Quick review of contact information. Are all phone numbers and emails still current? Has anyone left the company?
  • Quarterly: Review the full plan after each test. Update any procedures that didn't work as expected. Add any new systems to the inventory.
  • Annually: Complete review of the entire plan. Verify every section is current. Get sign-off from the business owner.
  • After any major change: New server, new office location, new cloud provider, new IT provider, staff changes in key roles — any of these trigger an immediate plan update.
